---
title: Notes to Paul Schrader's 'Notes on Film Noir'
date: 20 Mar 2026 3:13
---

- French Critics in 1946 observed that American films made during and after WW2 had an air of cynicism, pessimism and darkness that crept into the American cinema.
- This atmosphere was evident in both routine crime thrillers and melodramas. 
- Later on, by 1949, this ongoing trend in these films reached its peak. Their lighting grew darker; characters more corrupt; themes more fatalistic; tone more hopeless.
- Films before and around twenty years after this epoch dared not take such a harsh, uncomplimentary look at American life.
- As this essay was written in the 70's, we must note:
	- A trend in American cinema was arising—film noir is once again in vogue.
	- American movies once again looking at the underside of the American character
- However, compared to Kiss Me Deadly and Kiss Tomorrow Goodbye-two relentlessly cynical film noir- the new "self-hate" cinema of Easy Rider and Medium Cool come off naive and romantic. 
- During the ongoing rise in political cynicism of the 70's Schrader predicts film noir of the 40's increasing in attraction:
	The Forties may be to the Seventies what the Thirties were to the Sixties.
- Film noir also appealing to critics as they can explore excellent little-known films
	Schrader says: "film noir is oddly both one of Hollywood's best periods and least known".
- Also gives "auteur weary" critics a chance to delve into transdirectorial style.
- "What is a film noir?"
	- Not a genre.
	- Not defined as are western and gangster genres by conventions of setting/conflict, but by the more subtle qualities of tone/mood.
	- Film Noir is also a specific period of film history, like German Expressionism/French New Wave.
	- Film Noir refers to those Hollywood films of 40's/early 50's which portrayed the world of dark, slick city streets, crime and corruption.
	- Film Noir is a complexly organized period, it harks to many previous periods: 
		- Warner's 30's gangster films 
		- Carne and Duvivier's French "poetic realism"
		- Sternbergian melodrama
		- Farthest back, German Expressionist crime films (Lang's Mabuse cycle)
		- Film Noir's outer limits are from The Maltese Falcon (1941) to Touch of Evil (1958), and almost every dramatic Hollywood film from 1941-1953 contains noir elements.
	- Some foreign offshoots of Film Noir: The Third Man, Breathless, Le Doulos.
- Everyone has their own definition of Film Noir and when it dates
	- For example, not every film of urban nightlife is film noir
	- Film Noir need not be concerned with crime and corruption.
- Since film noir is defined by tone over genre, impossible to argue definitions of it against others.
- "How many noir elements does it take to make a Film Noir noir?"
- Schrader suggests there were four conditions in 40's Hollywood that birthed Film Noir:

#### 1.) War and Post-war Disillusionment

- Post-WW2 nihilism was a delayed reaction to the 30's.
- Through the Depression movies were used to keep spirits lit. For the most part they did.
- Crime films of this period were rags to riches and socially conscious. 
- Towards the end of the 30's darker crime films including You Only Live Once and The Roaring Twenties began to spring up. 
- If not for the War, film noir would have peaked much earlier in the 40's.
	- The need to produce Allied propaganda abroad and promote patriotism at home pushed back the efforts of film noir for a short while.
	- During the war the first uniquely Film Noir appeared: The Maltese Falcon, The Glass Key, This Gun For Hire and Laura. However these films lacked the "noir" bite the end of the war would bring.
- As soon as the war was over however, American films became markedly more sardonic + a boom in the crime film.
- Pushback against saccharine American cinema had built up for 15 years.
- Given the freedom, audiences/artists eager to have less optimistic view of things.
- Disillusionment with a return to peacetime economy was directly mirrored in sordidness of the urban crime film.
	- Films such as Cornered, The Blue Dahlia, Dead Reckoning and Ride A Pink Horse directly demonstrated this disillusionment. 
	- Servicemen returning to a cheating/dead spouse, business partner cheating him, society not worth fighting for were such themes.

### 2.) Post-war Realism

- Shortly after the war every film-producing country had a resurgence of realism.
	- Films were filmed on location rather than a studio. 
	- Realistic exteriors remained a permanent fixture of Film Noir.
- Realistic movement suited America's post-war mood: the public desired a more honest and harsh view of America. 
	- Studio streets could not suit such a desire.
- Trend of realism broke  Film Noir off from high-class melodrama, placing it in the streets with everyday people where it was a better fit.
- Pre-de Rochemont Film Noir definitely looks tamer than the post-War realistic films.

### 3.) The German Influence

- Many German filmmakers/technicians were hosted by Hollywood in 20's/30's.
- By the 40's they had integrated themselves in the Hollywood system.
- Germans were masters of the chiaroscuro. 
- Expressionist lighting has always lurked over Hollywood films, which makes its full bloom in Film Noir unsurprising.
- A large number of Germans/East Europeans working in noir also thus unsurprising. 
	- Names like Fritz Lang, Robert Siodmak, Billy Wilder, Franz Waxman.
- German expressionism relied on exterior studio lighting.
- This seems incompatible with post-war realistic exteriors.
- Unique quality of Film Noir is its ability to combine contradictory elements into a uniform style.
- Best noir technicians directed expressionistic/unnatural lighting onto realistic settings.
- Films like The Killers combine realism and expressionism perfectly.
- John Alton (cinematographer) perhaps the greatest master of noir and lighting to its full potential.

### 4.) The Hard-boiled Tradition

- "Hard-boiled" 30's writers like Hemingway, Hammett, Chandler, James M. Cain, Horace McCoy and John O'Hara created the "tough," a cynical way of acting and thinking which separated one from the world of everyday emotions—romanticism with a protective shell.
- Journalism/pulp fiction were the roots of such writers.
- Protagonists lived a narcissistic, defeatist code.
- When the 40's movies turned to the "tough" moral understrata, hard-boiled school was waiting with tropes, plots, dialogues, characters.
- These writers were as inspiring to noir screenwriting as Germans were for cinematography. 
- Chandler himself was Hollywood's most hardboiled writer, writing the script for Double Indemnity. Was the first film which played Film Noir for what it was: small-time, unredeemed, unheroic.

#### Stylistics

 - Some of Film Noir's recurring techniques:
	- Majority of scenes lit for night. Think midday offices with the shades pulled and lights off.
	- As in German expressionism, oblique/vertical lines are preferred to horizontal. These oppose horizontal American tradition of Ford/Griffith. Oblique lines tend to splinter a screen, making it restless/unstable. Light enters rooms of Film Noir in odd shapes like jagged trapezoids or vertical slits. T-Men is a great example of this.
	- Actors and setting given equal lighting emphasis. Characters are standing *in* the shadows. When the environment is given equal/greater weight than the actor it creates a fatalistic hopeless mood. The city will outlast and negate his best efforts.
	- Compositional tension is preferred to physical action.
	- Almost Freudian attachment to water. Lots of rain, docks, piers.
	- A love of romantic narration (Double Indemnity, The Postman Rings Twice etc.). Narration creates a mood of temps perdu (lost time): an irretrievable past, a predetermined fate and hopelessness. 
	- Chronological order is complex to reinforce feelings of hopelessness/lost time. This is to emphasize a noir principle: the how is always more important than the what.

#### Themes 

- Raymond Durgnat has 11 thematic categories for Film Noir.
- In each of his themes, frontierism has turned to paranoia/claustrophobia. Small-time gangsters now made it big. Young heroine is tired of taking a ride and now takes others for one.
- Durgnat however fails to to touch upon the potential over-riding noir theme: a passion for the past/present, but also fear of the future.
	- The noir hero dreads to look ahead and instead focuses on surviving day by day. If they cannot do that then they retreat to the past. 
	- Thus, noir's techniques emphasize loss, nostalgia, lack of clear priorities, insecurity, then submerge these self-doubts in mannerisms/style.
- Film Noir can be divided into three phases:
	1. The wartime period (1941-1946): the phase of the private eye and lone wolf, of Chandler, Hammett, and Greene, of Bogart and Bacall, Ladd and Lake, studio sets, more talk than action. Wilder/Chandler Double Indemnity came as a shock in 1944 and provided a bridge to post-War phase of noir.
	2. Post-War realistic period (1945-1949): these films tended more towards street crime, political corruption, police routine. 
	3. Final phase (1949-1953): psychotic action and suicidal impulse. The noir hero under years of pressure goes crazy. The psychotic killer, a subject worthy of study in the first period, a fringe threat in the second, was now the protagonist. This was the phase of "B" noir films, with psychoanalytically inclined directors such as Ray and Walsh. 
- The third phase is the peak of the Film Noir period. It was the most aesthetically/sociologically piercing. 
- The later noir films got down to the root causes of the period: loss of public honor, heroic conventions, personal integrity, psychic stability. 
- Kiss Me Deadly potentially the masterpiece of noir.
- With McCarthy and Eisenhower's rise, Americans wanted to see a more bourgeois view of themselves. Crime films moved to the suburbs. Criminals wore grey flannel suits, cops now a "mobile unit." Attempts at social criticism had to affirm American way of life. 
- Technically, television and its demand for full lighting/close-ups gradually undercut the German influence. Color cinematography was the final blow. 

---

- Film Noir was potentially the most creative period in Hollywood history. 
	- Median level of artistry very high.
	- A random Film Noir likely to be better made than a randomly selected silent comedy, musical, etc.
	- Film Noir gave artists a chance to work with previously forbidden themes, with conventions strong enough to protect mediocre directors.
	- Cinematographers became "stars."
- Film Noir was unrecognized for so long because it was considered a departure from the American character. Westerns were morally primitive, and gangster films Horatio Algeran (rags to riches) were considered more American than noir with its corruption and despair
- Film Noir suited "B" film, which were prone to attack from economic snobbery in critical circles. Praising a "B" film is seen as somehow slighting an "A" film.
- Fundamental reason for Film Noir's neglect: depends on choreography over sociology. American critics lag behind in analyzing visual style. American critics focus on theme over style, Noir focuses on style over theme.
- "American film critics have always been sociologists first and scientists second: film is important as it relates to large masses, and if a film goes awry it is often because the theme has been somehow "violated" by the style."
- Theme is hidden in style for Film Noir. Western and gangster films are easier to discern themes from stylistic analysis than noir.
